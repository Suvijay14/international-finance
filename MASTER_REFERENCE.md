# TOPIC 1: GLOBALIZATION AND THE MULTINATIONAL FIRM (IF1)

## Plain English Summary
International Finance is about doing business across countries. The moment money crosses a border, you face new risks: exchange rates can move against you, governments can change the rules, and markets don't work perfectly. But there are also huge opportunities — firms can produce where it's cheapest, raise money where it's cheapest, and sell everywhere.

## Four Key Dimensions of International Finance
1. **Foreign Exchange Risk** — Exchange rates between currencies fluctuate unpredictably. Profits made in a foreign currency can disappear when converted back to home currency.
2. **Political Risk** — Sovereign countries can change laws, impose taxes, or even expropriate foreign assets. No effective recourse.
3. **Market Imperfections** — Legal restrictions, transaction costs, information asymmetry, and discriminatory taxation prevent markets from working perfectly. This is WHY MNCs exist.
4. **Expanded Opportunity Set** — Firms can locate production anywhere, raise funds anywhere, and sell anywhere.

## Key Example: Foreign Exchange Risk
- You invest $1,000 in Toyota shares when $1 = ¥100
- Buy 50 shares at ¥2,000 each → ¥100,000 invested
- One year later, shares up 10% → worth ¥110,000
- But yen has depreciated: now $1 = ¥120
- Dollar value: ¥110,000 / 120 = **$916.67** ← You LOST money despite the share price rising!

## Key Example: Market Imperfections — Nestlé
- Nestlé had two share classes: bearer shares (foreigners only) and registered shares (Swiss only)
- Bearer shares were MORE expensive because of restricted supply to foreigners
- On November 18, 1988, Nestlé lifted restrictions → price spread between the two share types COLLAPSED immediately
- Lesson: market imperfections cause price distortions; when removed, prices equalize

## Multinational Corporation (MNC)
- A firm incorporated in one country with production/sales in others
- Benefits: economies of scale (spread R&D costs globally), access to underpriced labor, access to cheap capital

## Globalization Trends
1. Deregulation of financial markets
2. Financial innovations (currency futures, options, multi-currency bonds)
3. Trade liberalization
4. Privatization (state-owned → private ownership; improves efficiency by ~20%)
5. Global Financial Crisis 2008-2009

## Corporate Governance (brief intro)
- Agency problem: managers may act in their own interest, not shareholders'
- Corporate governance = legal/financial framework regulating manager-shareholder relationship
- More detailed in IF8

## Key Concept Box
> The goal of international financial management = **maximize shareholder wealth** while managing FX risk, political risk, and market imperfections.

# TOPIC 2: INTERNATIONAL MONETARY SYSTEM (IF2)

## Plain English Summary
The International Monetary System (IMS) is the set of rules that governs how currencies relate to each other and how countries make international payments. Think of it as the "operating system" for global trade.

## Evolution Timeline
| Period | System | Key Feature |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Before 1875 | Bimetallism | Gold AND silver used |
| 1875–1914 | Classical Gold Standard | All currencies = fixed weight of gold |
| 1915–1944 | Interwar Period | Collapse of gold standard, instability |
| 1945–1972 | Bretton Woods | Dollar pegged to gold; all others pegged to dollar |
| 1973–Present | Flexible Exchange Rates | Market-determined rates |

## Bretton Woods System (1945–1972)
- Named after July 1944 meeting in New Hampshire (44 nations)
- **Dollar-based gold exchange standard**: $35 = 1 ounce of gold. Other currencies pegged to USD.
- Only USD was fully convertible to gold
- Created: **IMF** (International Monetary Fund) and **World Bank**
- Advantage: stable exchange rates, lower transaction costs
- **Triffin Paradox** (why it collapsed): The US had to run a BOP DEFICIT to supply world with dollars as reserve currency → this undermined confidence in the dollar → system collapsed in early 1970s

## The Jamaica Agreement (1976)
- Flexible exchange rates officially accepted
- Gold officially **demonetized** (removed as reserve asset)
- Less-developed countries given greater IMF access

## Current Exchange Rate Arrangements (IMF Classification)
1. **Free Floating** — Market determines rate (USA, UK, Japan, Eurozone, Canada)
2. **Floating** — Largely market-determined but some intervention (Brazil, India, Turkey)
3. **Currency Board** — Local currency fully backed by foreign currency (Hong Kong, Bulgaria)
4. **Conventional Peg** — Fixed to another currency (Jordan, Saudi Arabia)
5. **No Separate Legal Tender** — Uses another country's currency (Ecuador, Panama use USD)
6. **Crawling Peg** — Adjusted gradually in small amounts (Honduras, Nicaragua)

## Fixed vs Flexible Exchange Rates
| Fixed | Flexible |
|-------|---------|
| Stability, predictable trade | Easy external adjustment |
| No policy autonomy | National policy autonomy |
| Need large reserves | No reserves needed |
| Risk of speculative attacks | Exchange rate uncertainty |

## Special Topics

### The Euro
- January 1, 1999: 11 EU countries adopted the euro (EMU created)
- January 1, 2002: Euro notes/coins introduced
- Managed by the **European Central Bank (ECB)** based in Frankfurt
- First time sovereign countries voluntarily gave up monetary independence

### Exchange Rate Crises
- **Mexican Peso Crisis** (1994): peso collapsed after political instability
- **Asian Currency Crisis** (1997): Thailand baht collapsed, spread across Asia
- **Argentine Peso Crisis** (2001): broke dollar peg, massive default

## Key Formula
> Under Bretton Woods: S(currency/USD) was fixed. Under floating: S is determined by market supply and demand.

# TOPIC 3: BALANCE OF PAYMENTS (IF3)

## Plain English Summary
The Balance of Payments (BoP) is like a country's bank statement — it records every financial transaction between a country's residents and the rest of the world over a period of time.

## Double-Entry Bookkeeping Rule
- **Credit (+)**: Transaction that brings money IN to the country (exports, foreign investment in the country)
- **Debit (−)**: Transaction that sends money OUT of the country (imports, investment abroad)

## The Four Accounts
### 1. Current Account (CA)
Records trade in real goods and services + transfers
- **Goods trade**: Exports/imports of physical goods (cars, oil, wheat)
- **Services**: Tourism, financial services, royalties, shipping
- **Primary Income**: Dividends, interest on foreign investments
- **Secondary Income**: Remittances, foreign aid (unrequited transfers)

### 2. Capital Account (KA)
- Transfers of ownership of assets (land, mineral rights, brand names)
- Usually small and negligible

### 3. Financial Account (FA)
Purchases/sales of financial assets across borders:
- **FDI** (Foreign Direct Investment): Buying enough shares to get control (usually >10%)
- **Portfolio Investment**: Stocks and bonds without control
- **Other Investment**: Bank deposits, trade credits, loans

### 4. Official Reserves Account (RA)
- Central bank's purchases/sales of international reserves
- Includes: Gold, foreign exchange, SDRs, IMF reserve positions
- Foreign exchange = ~96% of total reserves

## BoP Identity
**BCA + BKA + BFA = −BRA**
- Under **fixed exchange rates**: BRA ≠ 0 (central bank intervenes using reserves)
- Under **flexible exchange rates**: BRA ≈ 0 (no intervention needed; BCA + BFA = −BKA)

## U.S. BoP 2020 Key Numbers (from slides)
- Current Account Balance: **−$616.1 billion** (deficit)
- Goods: −$2,350.8 billion imports vs $1,428.8 billion exports
- Services: $705.6 billion exports vs −$460.3 billion imports
- Financial Account Balance: **+$662.0 billion** (surplus — foreigners invested more in US than US invested abroad)

## Important Concepts

### J-Curve Effect
After a currency depreciates:
- SHORT TERM: Trade balance WORSENS (imports cost more immediately, exports haven't increased yet)
- LONG TERM: Trade balance IMPROVES (exports increase, imports decrease)
- The path looks like a "J" shape

### Twin Deficit
If government runs a fiscal deficit, it often coincides with a current account deficit:
- Government spends more than it taxes → borrows from abroad → capital inflows → current account deficit

## Classic BoP Examples (from TD1)
**Q: A Japanese insurance company purchases U.S. Treasury bonds and pays from its NYC bank account.**
- Financial Account Credit (+): US sold financial asset to foreigner (cash inflow for USA)
- Financial Account Debit (−): Reduction in Japanese-owned bank deposit in NYC

**Q: A U.S. citizen eats at a Paris restaurant, pays with American Express.**
- Current Account Debit (−): U.S. imported a service (tourism)
- Financial Account Credit (+): Increase in U.S. liability (credit card debt)

**Q: Indian immigrant in LA sends check to parents in Mumbai.**
- Current Account Debit (−): Secondary income / remittance outflow
- Financial Account Credit (+): Decrease in U.S. bank deposit (offsetting entry)

## Key Formula Box
> **Trade Balance = Exports of Goods − Imports of Goods**
> **Current Account Balance = Trade Balance + Services + Primary Income + Secondary Income**
> **BoP Identity: BCA + BKA + BFA + BRA = 0**

# TOPIC 4: FOREIGN EXCHANGE MARKET (IF4)

## Plain English Summary
The FX market is where currencies are bought and sold. It's the LARGEST financial market in the world — $7.5 trillion traded every day in 2022. Unlike a stock exchange, it has no central location — it's a global 24-hour network.

## Key Terminology

### Exchange Rate
The price of one currency in terms of another.
- **Base currency**: The "1 unit" currency (e.g., EUR in EUR/USD = 1.36 means 1 EUR = 1.36 USD)
- **Quote currency**: How much of this you get per 1 base currency unit
- **Direct quote** (from home country perspective): price of 1 foreign currency in domestic currency
  - Example for American: EUR/USD = 1.12 → costs $1.12 to buy €1
- **Indirect quote**: price of 1 domestic currency in foreign currency
  - Example for American: USD/EUR = 0.89 → $1 buys €0.89
- **Direct = 1 / Indirect**

### Bid-Ask Spread
- **Bid**: Price the bank will BUY the base currency (always LOWER)
- **Ask/Offer**: Price the bank will SELL the base currency (always HIGHER)
- Spread = Ask − Bid → bank's profit
- Example: EUR/USD Bid: 1.1200 / Ask: 1.1205 → spread = 0.0005 ($0.0005 per euro)

## FX Market Structure: Two-Tier
1. **Interbank/Wholesale Market** (>90% of volume): Large banks trade with each other
2. **Client/Retail Market** (<10%): MNCs, fund managers, individual speculators

## Spot vs Forward Markets
- **Spot Rate**: Rate for immediate delivery (actually 2 business days)
- **Forward Rate**: Rate agreed TODAY for delivery at a future date (1 month, 3 months, 6 months, 1 year)

## Cross Rates
When you need to find the exchange rate between two currencies using a third (usually USD).
**Formula**: If you know $/A and $/B → then A/B = ($/B) / ($/A)

**Example**: 
- USD/GBP = 1.3447 (1 GBP = $1.3447)
- USD/EUR = 1.1235 (1 EUR = $1.1235)
- GBP/EUR = 1.1235 / 1.3447 = **0.8355** (1 GBP = 0.8355 EUR)

## Triangular Arbitrage
When cross rates are inconsistent, you can make risk-free profit by trading through 3 currencies.

**Example**:
- Given: USD/EUR = 1.1235, USD/GBP = 1.3447, EUR/GBP = 0.8350
- Check if consistent: EUR/GBP should = 1.1235/1.3447 = 0.8355
- Quoted rate is 0.8350 ≠ 0.8355 → **arbitrage opportunity!**
- Steps: Start with $, buy EUR, buy GBP with EUR, sell GBP back to $
- Profit = difference between actual and implied cross rate

**General Rule**: If quoted cross rate ≠ implied cross rate → arbitrage exists

## Forward Premium / Discount
If forward rate > spot rate: the base currency is at a **premium** (expected to appreciate)
If forward rate < spot rate: the base currency is at a **discount** (expected to depreciate)

**Formula (annualized)**:
Forward Premium/Discount = [(F − S) / S] × (360 / days) × 100%

**Example from TD3**:
- Spot: ZAR 11.0976/$, 3-month forward: ZAR 10.9308/$
- Dollar is at PREMIUM vs rand (forward rate for $ in ZAR is lower → dollar costs fewer rand in forward market... wait, actually dollar is SELLING at forward PREMIUM because 10.9308 < 11.0976 means fewer rand per dollar in forward → dollar appreciated in forward)
- Annualized discount for rand: 4 × (10.9308/11.0976 − 1) = −6.012%

## Key Data Points from Course
- Top traded currency: USD ($6,641 billion daily, 88% of all trades)
- Top currency pairs: EUR/USD, USD/JPY, GBP/USD
- UK is largest FX market by country (38% of global turnover in 2022)

## Practical Example: Australian Woolgrower
- Australian producer sells AUD 1,300,000 of wool to Canadian buyer
- Exchange rate: 1 AUD = 0.98112 CAD
- Canadian dollar cost: 1,300,000 × 0.98112 = **CAD 1,275,456**

# TOPIC 5: PPP AND INTEREST RATE PARITY (IF5)

## Plain English Summary
This topic is about the "law of one price" — the idea that identical things should cost the same everywhere. When applied to currencies, it gives us powerful tools to predict exchange rates.

## Law of One Price (LOP)
The same good should sell for the same price everywhere (when expressed in the same currency). If not → arbitrage opportunity.

## Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)

### Absolute PPP
Exchange rate should equal the ratio of price levels between two countries.
**S = P_domestic / P_foreign**

**Big Mac Index** (The Economist): Uses burger price to test PPP — if a Big Mac costs $5 in the US and €4 in Germany, then the "PPP rate" is 5/4 = $1.25/€. If actual rate differs → currency is over/undervalued.

### Relative PPP
Exchange rate changes reflect **inflation rate differentials** between countries.

**Formula**: (S_t+1 − S_t) / S_t ≈ π_domestic − π_foreign

Or equivalently: **Expected exchange rate = S × (1 + π_d) / (1 + π_f)**

**Example**:
- Current rate: $1.20/€
- US inflation: 3%, Eurozone inflation: 1%
- PPP predicted rate in 1 year: 1.20 × (1.03/1.01) = **$1.2238/€**
- Dollar depreciates (takes more dollars to buy euros) because US has higher inflation

**Intuition**: If US has higher inflation, US goods become relatively more expensive → dollar should weaken to restore competitiveness.

## Interest Rate Parity (IRP)

### The Core Idea
You can invest $1 two ways:
1. In the US at dollar interest rate: get $1 × (1 + i_$)
2. Convert to pounds, invest at pound rate, sell pounds forward: get $1 × (F/S) × (1 + i_£)

**In equilibrium, both must give the same result**:
**(1 + i_$) = (F/S) × (1 + i_£)**

Or: **F = S × (1 + i_$) / (1 + i_£)**

### Covered Interest Arbitrage (CIA)
When IRP doesn't hold → risk-free profit available.

**Famous Example from IF5**:
- US rate: 5%, UK rate: 8%
- Spot: $1.80/£, Forward: $1.78/£
- Check IRP: (1.78/1.80) × 1.08 = 1.068 > 1.05 → IRP violated! UK is better after hedging
- Strategy: Borrow $1,000,000 at 5% in US → Buy £555,556 spot → Invest at 8% → Sell £600,000 forward at $1.78
- Profit: $1,068,000 − $1,050,000 = **$18,000 risk-free!**

### IRP Exercise (from slides)
- US: 5%, Ruritania: 15.5%, Spot: RUP 50/$, Forward: RUP 55/$
- US investment: $1,000 × 1.05 = $1,050
- RUP investment: $1,000 → 50,000 RUP × 1.155 = 57,750 RUP / 55 = $1,050
- **Equal! → IRP holds → No arbitrage**

### Covered vs Uncovered IRP
- **Covered**: Uses forward contract to eliminate FX risk
- **Uncovered**: Assumes forward rate = expected future spot rate (riskier, speculative)

## Currency Carry Trade
- Borrow in low-interest-rate country, invest in high-interest-rate country WITHOUT hedging
- Profits if the high-yield currency does NOT depreciate as much as IRP predicts
- RISK: If high-yield currency suddenly collapses → massive losses

## Fisher Effect
Real interest rate = Nominal interest rate − Inflation rate
(1 + r_nominal) = (1 + r_real) × (1 + π)

**International Fisher Effect**: Countries with higher inflation will have higher nominal interest rates, and their currencies will depreciate accordingly.

## All Parity Relationships (the "wheel")
```
PPP connects: exchange rates ↔ inflation rates
IRP connects: exchange rates ↔ interest rates
Fisher connects: interest rates ↔ inflation rates
UIP connects: forward rates ↔ expected future spot rates
```

## Key Formula Box
> **Relative PPP**: E[S(t+1)] = S(t) × (1 + π_d)/(1 + π_f)
> **IRP**: F = S × (1 + i_d)/(1 + i_f)
> **Check for CIA**: Compare (F/S) × (1 + i_f) vs (1 + i_d) — if not equal, arbitrage exists
> **Forward Premium**: (F − S)/S × 12/months × 100 (annualized)

# TOPIC 6: FUTURES AND OPTIONS (IF6)

## Plain English Summary
Derivatives are financial contracts whose value depends on an underlying asset (a currency, commodity, stock index). They can be used either to HEDGE (reduce risk) or SPECULATE (take risk for profit). You need to know futures AND options inside out.

## Spot vs Forward vs Futures
| Feature | Spot | Forward | Futures |
|---------|------|---------|---------|
| When traded? | Now | OTC, now | Exchange |
| Delivery | Immediate | At maturity | Rarely (exit early) |
| Standardized? | N/A | No (custom) | Yes |
| Counterparty risk | Yes | Yes | No (clearinghouse) |
| Daily settlement? | N/A | No | Yes (marking-to-market) |

## Futures Contracts

### Key Features
- **Standardized**: Fixed contract sizes, fixed delivery dates
- **Exchange-traded**: CME Group (Chicago Mercantile Exchange)
- **Clearinghouse**: Acts as counterparty to BOTH sides → eliminates default risk
- **Performance Bond (Margin)**: ~2% of contract value deposited upfront
- **Daily Resettlement (Mark-to-Market)**: Every day, gains/losses settled in cash

### Daily Resettlement Logic
- Price goes UP → Long position (buyer) WINS, Short position (seller) LOSES
- Price goes DOWN → Short position WINS, Long position LOSES
- Cash transferred DAILY from loser to winner through margin account

### Futures Pricing Formula (same as forward pricing using IRP)
**F = S₀ × (1 + r_domestic)^T / (1 + r_foreign)^T**

Or for commodities: **F = S₀ × (1 + r_f − convenience yield + storage cost)^T**

**Example from TD5**:
- S₀ = 95, r_f = 10%, convenience yield = 8/95, T = 0.5 years
- F = 95 × [1 + 0.10 − (8/95)]^0.5 = **95.747**

### Futures P&L
- **Long (buyer)**: Profit = (Settlement Price − Futures Price) × Contract Size
- **Short (seller)**: Profit = (Futures Price − Settlement Price) × Contract Size

**Example (from exam prep)**:
- Buy gold futures at $1,500/oz, contract = 100 oz
- At maturity, gold = $1,600/oz
- Profit = ($1,600 − $1,500) × 100 = **$10,000**

### Speculation with Futures
**Example (from exam prep)**:
- EUR futures at $1.10, you expect EUR to fall to $1.05
- Take SHORT position in 4 contracts (125,000 EUR each)
- Profit = ($1.10 − $1.05) × 125,000 × 4 = **$25,000**

## Options Contracts

### What is an Option?
A right (NOT obligation) to buy or sell an asset at a fixed price (strike/exercise price) before or at expiry.

**Call Option**: Right to BUY
**Put Option**: Right to SELL

**American option**: Can be exercised any time before expiry
**European option**: Can only be exercised AT expiry

### Option Positions
| Position | You... | You profit when... |
|----------|--------|-------------------|
| Long Call | Buy the right to buy | Asset price RISES above strike |
| Short Call | Sell the right to buy | Asset price stays BELOW strike |
| Long Put | Buy the right to sell | Asset price FALLS below strike |
| Short Put | Sell the right to sell | Asset price stays ABOVE strike |

### Option Payoff at Maturity
- **Call payoff** = max(S_T − K, 0) where K = strike price, S_T = spot at maturity
- **Put payoff** = max(K − S_T, 0)
- **Net profit** = Payoff − Premium paid (for buyer)

### Premium (Price of Option)
What makes an option more expensive?

**Call option premium increases when:**
- Underlying price ↑ (more likely to be in the money)
- Strike price ↓ (easier to make profit)
- Time to maturity ↑ (more time = more chance of profit)
- Volatility/Standard deviation ↑ (more chance of big move)
- Risk-free rate ↑ (PV of strike is lower)

**Put option premium increases when:**
- Underlying price ↓ (more likely to be in the money)
- Strike price ↑ (higher threshold → easier to profit)
- Time to maturity ↑
- Volatility ↑
- Risk-free rate ↓ (from slides: "Increase of risk-free rate → increase of put price" — note this is debated; standard Black-Scholes says higher rates → lower put price, but the course slides say opposite. USE THE COURSE SLIDE answer: higher risk-free rate → more expensive put)

**From course slides (exam answer):**
Put option becomes MORE EXPENSIVE when:
1. Increase of exercise/strike price
2. Increase of time to maturity
3. Increase of standard deviation (volatility)
4. **Decrease** of underlying price
5. Increase of risk-free rate ← (use this answer per course slides)

### Hedging with Options

**Example: U.S. firm with Mexican Peso receivable (Q9 from past paper)**
- U.S. firm will receive MXN 20,000,000 in 1 year
- Buys PUT options on MXN: strike = MXN 20/USD (= $0.05/MXN)
- Premium = $0.001/MXN
- Maturity spot: MXN 18/USD (= $0.0556/MXN)
- Since market rate ($0.0556) > strike implied ($0.05), option NOT exercised (spot is better)
- Convert at spot: 20,000,000 / 18 = **$1,111,111.11**
- Premium cost: $0.001 × 20,000,000 = $20,000, with 5% interest = **$21,000**
- Net proceeds: $1,111,111.11 − $21,000 = **$1,090,111.11**

### Option Strategies
**Protective Put**: Own shares + buy put → limits downside while keeping upside
**Protective Call** (from exam): Own shares + buy call... actually protective call = own cash + buy call (called "fiduciary call") or it can mean: buy put to protect shares. Check course definition: "An investor who owns shares will simultaneously purchase a call option on that same stock" — this is unusual; normally protective PUT. USE COURSE DEFINITION.

### Open Interest
Total number of outstanding long OR short contracts for a particular delivery month. Indicates market activity and liquidity.

## Key Formula Box
> **Futures Price**: F = S × (1 + r_d)^T / (1 + r_f)^T
> **Futures P&L (Long)**: (F_close − F_open) × Contract Size
> **Call Payoff**: max(S_T − K, 0)
> **Put Payoff**: max(K − S_T, 0)
> **Net Option Profit (buyer)**: Payoff − Premium × (1 + r)^T [adjusted for time value]

# TOPIC 7: MANAGEMENT OF TRANSACTION EXPOSURE (IF7)

## Plain English Summary
When a company has a contract in a foreign currency, it faces the risk that exchange rates will move before it gets paid. This is "exposure." This topic covers 3 types of exposure and how to hedge each one.

## Three Types of Exposure

### 1. Transaction Exposure
**Definition**: Risk that the value of a fixed foreign-currency contract (receivable or payable) will change due to exchange rate movements.
**When it happens**: Any time a firm has a foreign currency receivable or payable.
**Example**: Boeing sells a landing gear to British Airways for £10 million, payable in one year. The dollar value is uncertain.

### 2. Economic Exposure (Operating Exposure)
**Definition**: Extent to which the firm's OVERALL VALUE can be affected by unexpected exchange rate changes (beyond just the current contracts — affects future cash flows too).
**Example**: A U.S. car manufacturer competes with Japanese cars. If yen depreciates, Japanese cars become cheaper → U.S. firm loses sales even if it has no yen contracts.
**Harder to hedge** than transaction exposure.

### 3. Translation Exposure (Accounting Exposure)
**Definition**: Risk that a firm's consolidated financial statements are affected by exchange rate changes when translating foreign subsidiaries' accounts into the home currency.
**No actual cash flow impact** — purely an accounting effect.
**Example**: If a U.S. firm has a German subsidiary with €100M in assets, and the euro weakens from $1.20 to $1.10, the US balance sheet shows €100M × $1.10 = $110M instead of $120M → $10M "loss" even though no cash changed hands.

## Should the Firm Hedge?
**Arguments FOR hedging:**
1. Information asymmetry: managers know risks better than shareholders
2. Differential transaction costs: firms can hedge cheaper than individual shareholders
3. Default costs: hedging reduces probability of bankruptcy → lower cost of capital
4. Progressive corporate taxes: stable earnings → lower average tax rate than volatile earnings

**Argument AGAINST (in perfect markets):** In Modigliani-Miller world with no taxes, no transaction costs, and no information asymmetry, hedging adds no value because shareholders can hedge themselves. But real world has these imperfections → hedging IS valuable.

## Hedging Methods for Transaction Exposure

### 1. Forward Market Hedge
Sell/buy foreign currency forward to lock in exchange rate.

**Boeing Example (Receivable — most important)**:
- Boeing has £10M receivable in 1 year
- US rate: 6.10%, UK rate: 9%, Spot: $1.50/£, Forward: $1.46/£
- **Hedge**: Sell £10M forward at $1.46/£
- Locked-in dollar proceeds: £10M × $1.46 = **$14.6 million** (certain!)
- If spot at maturity = $1.40: would have gotten $14M unhedged → hedge SAVED $600K
- If spot at maturity = $1.50: would have gotten $15M unhedged → hedge COST $400K
- **Gain/Loss from hedge = Forward proceeds − Spot proceeds = $14.6M − S_T × £10M**

### 2. Money Market Hedge
Borrow in foreign currency against receivable, convert to home currency, invest at home rate.

**Boeing Example (Receivable)**:
- Has £10M receivable in 1 year
- Borrow PV of £10M at UK rate: £10M / 1.09 = **£9,174,312**
- Convert £9,174,312 to USD at spot $1.50/£: **= $13,761,468**
- Invest $13,761,468 at US rate 6.10% for 1 year: **= $14,600,000**
- After 1 year: receive £10M, use it to repay pound loan
- **Result: Same $14.6M as forward hedge** (IRP ensures this in equilibrium)

### 3. Options Hedge
Buy put options on the foreign currency (for a receivable) to protect against currency decline while keeping upside potential.

**Key advantage over forward**: If the foreign currency STRENGTHENS, you can let the option expire and benefit from better spot rate.
**Key disadvantage**: You must pay a premium upfront.

**Example**:
- Boeing has £10M receivable, buys put at $1.46/£, pays premium
- If spot = $1.40: exercise put → get $14.6M (protected)
- If spot = $1.55: let put expire → convert at spot → get $15.5M (upside captured!)

### 4. Currency Futures Hedge
Similar to forward but uses standardized exchange-traded contracts.
**Disadvantage vs forward**: 
1. Standardized sizes mean imprecise hedging
2. Daily mark-to-market creates uncertain interim cash flows

## Hedging Payables (the reverse)
For a foreign currency PAYABLE (you OWE foreign currency):
- **Forward hedge**: BUY foreign currency forward (lock in rate you'll pay)
- **Money market**: Borrow at home, convert to foreign, invest at foreign rate, use at maturity to pay
- **Options**: Buy CALL options on the foreign currency (right to buy at strike)

## Comparing Hedging Methods
From TD8 (Exercise 9):
The best method depends on your view:
- **Maximum certainty**: Forward hedge (locked-in rate, no upside/downside)
- **Flexibility + protection**: Options hedge (costs premium, but keeps upside)
- **Eliminate risk**: Money market hedge (same economic result as forward if IRP holds)

## Key Formula Box
> **Forward Hedge Gain/Loss** = (F − S_T) × FC Amount (for receivable)
> **Money Market Hedge**: Borrow FC Amount / (1 + i_f), convert at S, invest at i_d
> **Option Hedge Net Proceeds** = max(K × Amount, S_T × Amount) − Premium × (1 + r)^T

# TOPIC 8: CORPORATE GOVERNANCE (IF8)

## Plain English Summary
Corporate governance is about WHO controls a company and HOW they use that control. The central problem is: when you (shareholder) hire a manager to run your company, how do you make sure they don't steal from you or waste your money? This is the "agency problem."

## Key Definitions
- **Corporate Governance**: The economic, legal, and institutional framework in which corporate control and cash flow rights are distributed among shareholders, managers, and other stakeholders.
- **Agency Problem**: Conflict of interest between managers (agents) and shareholders (principals). Managers might act in their OWN short-term interest instead of shareholder's long-term interest.
- **Residual Control Rights**: The right to make decisions in situations NOT covered by explicit contracts. Managers exercise substantial discretion over investors' capital.

## The Agency Problem — What Can Go Wrong
1. Managers consume excessive perks (private jets, luxury offices)
2. Managers steal funds (transfer pricing schemes, related-party transactions)
3. Managers waste money on unprofitable pet projects
4. Managers adopt anti-takeover measures to protect their jobs
5. Managers manipulate accounting to inflate their bonuses

## Remedies for the Agency Problem (8 mechanisms)
1. **Independent Board of Directors** — Board members with no ties to management can monitor effectively; best practice: hold regular meetings WITHOUT management present
2. **Incentive Contracts** — Stock options, performance bonuses align manager interests with shareholders; some CEOs take $1 salary but earn millions in stock (e.g., Steve Jobs, Elon Musk)
3. **Concentrated Ownership** — Large shareholders (>10%) have strong incentive to monitor management
4. **Accounting Transparency** — Better disclosure reduces information asymmetry; harder for managers to "cook the books"
5. **Debt** — Forces managers to pay creditors; bankruptcy threat disciplines managers; forces cash distribution (prevents empire-building)
6. **Shareholder Activism** — Activist investors (hedge funds, pension funds) push for governance changes (e.g., Toshiba 2021)
7. **Overseas Stock Listings** — Listing on NYSE/LSE subjects foreign firms to higher governance standards
8. **Market for Corporate Control** — Threat of takeover disciplines managers; underperforming firms get acquired

## Corporate Governance Around the World

### Ownership Concentration
- **USA/UK (Anglo-Saxon)**: Dispersed ownership — many small shareholders, no dominant owner
  - Average of 3 largest shareholders in US: 19% (very dispersed)
  - USA: Agency problem is mainly **manager vs. shareholder**
- **Europe (Germany, France, Italy)**: Concentrated ownership — families, banks, government
  - Germany: 48% avg ownership by 3 largest shareholders
  - Agency problem is mainly **large shareholder vs. minority shareholder**
- **Asia (Japan, China, Korea)**: Cross-holdings, government, families
  - Japan: Keiretsu — network of cross-shareholding firms
  - China: State-owned enterprises dominate

### Mechanisms in Different Countries
- **Germany**: Banks and insurance companies as major shareholders; two-tier board (supervisory + management board)
- **Japan**: Keiretsu cross-holdings; insider-dominated boards
- **China**: Government ownership; SOEs listed on exchanges
- **Asia/Latin America**: Founder families control; pyramidal ownership structures

## Concentrated Ownership Mechanisms (key exam topic!)

### 1. Shares with Superior Voting Rights (Dual-Class Shares)
- Some shares get MORE votes per share (e.g., 10 votes vs. 1 vote for regular shares)
- Founder keeps control even with minority economic ownership
- Allows raising capital while retaining control

### 2. Pyramidal Ownership Structures
- Company A owns controlling stake in Company B, which owns controlling stake in Company C
- With just enough shares at each level to control, a single investor controls a chain with small investment
- **Example**: Family owns 51% of A, A owns 51% of B, B owns 51% of C
  - Family's effective ownership of C = 51% × 51% × 51% = only 13.3%
  - But family CONTROLS C through the pyramid

### 3. Interfirm Cross-Holdings
- Company A owns shares in Company B, and Company B owns shares in Company A
- Mutual reinforcement of control; makes takeovers very difficult
- Common in Japan (keiretsu) and historically in Germany

## Investor Protection and the Law (La Porta et al.)
Countries with **better investor protection** (stronger legal systems) have:
- More **dispersed** ownership (shareholders trust the system)
- Larger and more developed **capital markets**
- Higher **company valuations** (Tobin's Q)
- More **IPOs** and equity market development
- Higher **economic growth**

Countries with **weak investor protection** have:
- More **concentrated ownership** (because legal system can't protect you, so you protect yourself by being the large shareholder)
- Concentrated ownership is a SUBSTITUTE for weak legal protection

**Legal system origin matters**:
- **Common law** (UK, USA, Australia): Better shareholder protection
- **Civil law** (France, Germany, Italy, Japan): Generally weaker minority shareholder protection

## Corporate Governance Reform
Major reforms after scandals (Enron 2001, WorldCom 2002, Parmalat 2003):
- **Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX) 2002** in USA: CEO/CFO must personally certify financial statements; heavier penalties for fraud
- **International convergence**: Move toward common standards globally

## Calculation: Ownership Percentage
**Q: Company A has 10,000 shares. Investor B holds 4,000 shares with superior voting rights. What is Investor B's ownership?**
- Ownership = 4,000 / 10,000 = **40%**
- Even with 40% economic ownership, if shares have superior voting rights (say 2:1), Investor B has 8,000 / (6,000 + 8,000) = **57% of votes**
- This concentrated ownership with superior rights SUBSTITUTES for weak legal protection because Investor B can directly monitor management

## Calculation: Control Value
**Q: Dominant investor holds 60% of voting rights. Company value = $10M. Investor has $2M debt. Calculate control value C.**
- Value of shares controlled = 60% × $10M = **$6M**
- Subtract debt obligations = $6M − $2M = **$4M control value**
- Control value = voting share × total value − debt obligations

## Case Studies (from HW9 and TD9)

### Petrobras Scandal (Brazil)
- Brazil's state-controlled oil company
- Contractors bribed Petrobras executives; politicians involved; "Operation Car Wash"
- At least $1.7 billion diverted through inflated contracts
- Odebrecht settled for $3.5 billion — largest anticorruption settlement in history
- **Lesson**: Government ownership → weak corporate governance → massive corruption
- Former presidents da Silva and Rousseff implicated
- **Governance lesson**: Government-controlled firms need EXTRA independent oversight; free press and privatization as solutions

### Parmalat (Italy) — "Europe's Enron"
- Italian dairy company; expanded aggressively in 1990s to 30 countries
- $4.9 billion cash reserve simply did NOT EXIST (fake Bank of America account)
- Total debt was €16 billion, more than DOUBLE what was on the balance sheet
- Managers "invented assets" for 15 years before collapse (2003)
- **Why it lasted so long**: Italy has LOW accounting transparency; family-controlled with no independent board; auditors (Grant Thornton, Deloitte) failed due diligence
- **Governance lesson**: Accounting transparency is essential; independent auditors matter; family control without oversight = disaster

### Enron vs. Bombay Politicians (India)
- Enron invested $2.9 billion in Indian power plant (Dabhol) in 1992
- Made political miscalculation: didn't account for nationalist backlash
- BJP won 1995 election and canceled the project
- Enron had to renegotiate → cut costs from $2.9B to $2.5B, give state utility 30% stake
- **Lesson**: Political risk is real; must assess ALL political stakeholders before FDI; build in political risk assessment

## Key Formula Box
> **Ownership % = Shares held / Total shares outstanding × 100**
> **Effective control through pyramid = stake_level1 × stake_level2 × stake_level3...**
> **Control Value C = (voting %) × (Company Value) − Debt Obligations**

# TOPIC 9: FOREIGN DIRECT INVESTMENT & CROSS-BORDER M&A (IF9)

## Plain English Summary
FDI is when a company invests in another country in a way that gives it CONTROL (unlike portfolio investment which is just owning shares). Companies do this through building new facilities (Greenfield) or buying existing companies (M&A). But why do they do it? And what are the risks?

## FDI vs Portfolio Investment
- **FDI**: Acquire CONTROL (typically >10% threshold) — your company makes decisions about operations
- **Portfolio**: Buy stocks/bonds for return, NO control over operations

## Global FDI Trends (from slides)
- World FDI stock: $514 billion (1980) → $7,400 billion (2000) → **$39,000 billion (2020)** — explosive growth!
- Top FDI source countries (2015-2020 outflows): Japan, China, United States
- Top FDI destination countries (2015-2020 inflows): United States, China, United Kingdom
- Top 10 countries = ~64% of outflows; ~51% of inflows

## Why Do Firms Invest Overseas? (6 Theories)

### 1. Trade Barriers
- Governments impose tariffs, quotas, and import restrictions
- Solution: Move production INSIDE the protected market to avoid trade barriers
- **Classic Example**: Honda built factories in Ohio so its cars would NOT face US import tariffs/quotas
- Natural trade barriers also exist: transportation costs (bulky goods like cement, mineral ore)

### 2. Imperfect Labor Market
- Labor markets are the MOST imperfect because workers can't freely cross borders (immigration restrictions)
- Persistent wage differentials across countries
- Solution: Instead of importing workers, EXPORT the factory to cheap-labor locations
- **Evidence** (2020 average hourly manufacturing wages):
  - Denmark: $54.46, Germany: $47.52, USA: $29.08
  - China: $5.77, Vietnam: $1.82, India: $0.80
  - → Huge incentive to move production to developing countries

### 3. Intangible Assets
- MNCs have proprietary advantages: technology, brand, patents, management know-how
- Problem with licensing: foreigners might steal/misappropriate your intangible assets
- Solution: FDI allows using intangible assets globally while PROTECTING them
- **Internalization Theory**: Better to own foreign operations than license them
- **Classic Example**: Coca-Cola withdrew from India (1970s) rather than reveal its secret formula to the government. Returned to India only in 1990s when laws were more favorable.

### 4. Vertical Integration
- **Backward vertical FDI**: Invest in sources of raw materials/inputs (secure supply at stable prices)
- **Forward vertical FDI**: Invest in distribution/sales channels closer to end customers
- Also: build processing/manufacturing near natural resources to save transportation costs
- **Example**: Oil companies own the wells (backward) AND the gas stations (forward)
- Benefit: Can create barrier to entry for competitors if you control key resources

### 5. Product Life Cycle Theory
The location of production changes as a product matures:
1. **Introduction**: Production at home (close to customers, need flexibility)
2. **Growth**: Export to foreign markets as demand develops
3. **Maturity**: Product becomes standardized; cost becomes critical → FDI to cut costs
4. **Decline**: Production moves to lowest-cost locations (often developing countries)
- **Classic Example**: Personal computers — invented in USA, production moved to Taiwan/China as product matured

### 6. Shareholder Diversification Services
- If capital markets have barriers (restrictions on foreign investment), shareholders can't diversify globally themselves
- MNCs that have international operations provide INDIRECT diversification to shareholders
- A share in a US MNC is partially a bet on Germany, Japan, etc.
- Value decreases as capital markets open up (shareholders can do it themselves)

## FDI Entry Modes: Greenfield vs M&A

### Greenfield Investment
- Build a brand-new facility from scratch in the foreign country
- **Advantages**: 
  - Full control over design, technology, corporate culture
  - No inherited problems (bad labor relations, outdated equipment)
  - Often eligible for government incentives
- **Disadvantages**:
  - Takes time (years) to build and get operational
  - High upfront cost
  - Must build market position from zero

### Cross-Border Merger & Acquisition (M&A)
- Buy an existing foreign company (merger = two become one; acquisition = one buys the other)
- **Advantages**:
  - Immediate market access (brand, customers, distribution already exist)
  - Faster than Greenfield
  - Acquire local management expertise and knowledge
- **Disadvantages**:
  - Often overpay (winner's curse; competition among bidders drives up price)
  - Integration challenges (cultural clash, systems integration)
  - May inherit problems (debt, labor issues, legal liabilities)
  - Many studies show M&As destroy VALUE for acquirer shareholders (seller wins!)

## Political Risk and FDI (very important!)

### Types of Political Risk
1. **Transfer risk**: Government restricts repatriation of profits/capital
2. **Operational risk**: Regulations change, price controls, labor laws
3. **Ownership risk**: Expropriation, nationalization of foreign assets
4. **Reputational risk**: Being associated with a politically sensitive country

### Managing Political Risk
1. **Negotiation before investment**: Concession agreements, stabilization clauses
2. **Political risk insurance**: Through private insurers or World Bank's MIGA (Multilateral Investment Guarantee Agency)
3. **Local financing/joint ventures**: Share risk with local partner; harder to expropriate
4. **Local stakeholder management**: Hire local managers, work with local communities

### Enron India Case — Key Political Risk Lessons
1. **Underestimated political opposition**: Enron focused on Congress party, ignored BJP opposition
2. **No competitive bidding**: Fast-tracked deal without tender → looked corrupt/unfair to public
3. **Confidentiality backfired**: Hiding contract details fueled corruption accusations
4. **U.S. government pressure backfired**: BJP used it as "foreign bullying" → political gain
5. **Lesson**: Assess ALL political stakeholders; transparent processes prevent backlash; build local partnerships

## Key Concepts: Control vs Cash Flow Rights
A dominant investor may have:
- **Voting rights** (control): Determined by shares with voting power
- **Cash flow rights** (economic interest): Actual economic ownership percentage
- Through dual-class shares and pyramids: VOTING RIGHTS > CASH FLOW RIGHTS
- This separation is the root of the large shareholder vs. minority shareholder conflict

## Key Formula Box
> **FDI Control Threshold**: Typically >10% ownership = FDI (vs. portfolio)
> **Effective control in pyramid**: Multiply ownership stakes at each level
> **Control premium in M&A**: Acquirer pays above market price; premium = (acquisition price − pre-bid price) / pre-bid price × 100%

## Section A: Computation Questions

### Q1: Forward Hedge (from exam screenshots)
**A multinational corporation wants to hedge 300,000 EUR. Spot EUR/USD = 1.10, 6-month forward = 1.15. Calculate the hedging cost.**

**Answer**:
- Amount at forward rate: 300,000 × 1.15 = **$345,000** (cost in USD using forward)
- Amount at spot rate: 300,000 × 1.10 = **$330,000** (cost at spot)
- Hedging cost = $345,000 − $330,000 = **$15,000**
- The corporation pays $15,000 more by locking in the forward rate vs. spot, but gains certainty against EUR appreciation above 1.15.

### Q2: Ownership Percentage
**Company A has 10,000 shares. Investor B holds 4,000 shares with superior voting rights (2 votes per share). What is ownership % and how does it serve as substitute for weak governance?**

**Answer**:
- Economic ownership = 4,000 / 10,000 = **40%**
- Voting power = (4,000 × 2) / (6,000 × 1 + 4,000 × 2) = 8,000 / 14,000 = **57.1% of votes**
- Substitute for weak governance: In countries with weak legal protection of minority shareholders, concentrated ownership with superior voting rights allows large shareholders to directly monitor and control management, reducing the agency problem without relying on courts.

### Q3: Control Value Calculation
**Dominant investor holds 60% voting rights. Company value = $10M. Investor has $2M in debt. Calculate control value C.**

**Answer**:
- Share of company value controlled = 60% × $10M = $6M
- Subtract debt: $6M − $2M = **C = $4M**

### Q4: Futures P&L
**Enter gold futures contract to buy at $1,500/oz. At maturity, spot = $1,600/oz. Contract size = 100 oz. Profit/loss?**

**Answer**:
- Long position (you agreed to BUY at $1,500)
- Profit = (Spot − Futures) × Size = ($1,600 − $1,500) × 100 = **$10,000 profit**

### Q5: Speculation with Futures
**EUR futures at $1.10, contract size 125,000 EUR. You expect EUR to fall to $1.05. Take position in 4 contracts. Expected profit?**

**Answer**:
- Expectation: EUR will fall → Take SHORT position (agree to SELL EUR at $1.10)
- If correct and spot = $1.05:
- Profit = ($1.10 − $1.05) × 125,000 × 4 = $0.05 × 500,000 = **$25,000**

### Q6: Forward Hedge P&L (TechGlobal)
**TechGlobal invoices ¥800M payable in 1 year. Forward rate: $0.0095/¥. Expected spot: $0.0100/¥.**

**Answer**:
- Hedged (forward): ¥800M × $0.0095 = $7,600,000
- Unhedged (expected spot): ¥800M × $0.0100 = $8,000,000
- Gain/loss from hedging = $7.6M − $8.0M = **−$400,000 (opportunity cost of hedging)**
- Note: This is not a loss — hedging reduces uncertainty; the $400K is the cost of the insurance against yen weakening

### Q7: IRP Check
**US rate: 5%, Ruritania rate: 15.5%, Spot: RUP 50/$, Forward: RUP 55/$. Do arbitrage opportunities exist?**

**Answer**:
- US investment: $1,000 × 1.05 = **$1,050**
- Ruritanian investment: $1,000 × 50 = 50,000 RUP → × 1.155 = 57,750 RUP → / 55 = **$1,050**
- Both give same result → **IRP holds → No arbitrage opportunity**

### Q8: Covered Interest Arbitrage
**US rate: 5%, UK rate: 8%, Spot: $1.80/£, Forward: $1.78/£. Is there arbitrage?**

**Answer**:
- Check IRP: F should = S × (1 + i_US)/(1 + i_UK) = 1.80 × (1.05/1.08) = **$1.75/£**
- Actual forward = $1.78 > $1.75 → IRP violated → **Arbitrage exists!**
- Strategy: Borrow in US (cheaper), invest in UK (higher after adjusting for FX)
- Borrow $1,000,000 at 5% → repay $1,050,000
- Buy £555,556 at $1.80/£
- Invest at 8% → £600,000 at maturity
- Sell £600,000 forward at $1.78 → **$1,068,000**
- Arbitrage profit: $1,068,000 − $1,050,000 = **$18,000**

## Section B: Open-Ended Questions & Model Answers

### Q: Discuss the Agency Problem and how incentive contracts alleviate it.

**Model Answer**:
The agency problem arises from the separation of ownership and control in public corporations. When professional managers (agents) run companies on behalf of shareholders (principals), their interests may not align. Managers might pursue their own short-term interests — such as consuming excessive perquisites, engaging in empire-building, or protecting their jobs through anti-takeover measures — rather than maximizing long-term shareholder value. This is exacerbated when managers hold small equity stakes and shareholders are too dispersed to monitor effectively.

Incentive contracts address this by aligning managerial interests with those of shareholders. Stock options, for instance, only pay off when the share price rises — giving managers a direct financial stake in the company's success. Performance-related bonuses tied to long-term metrics (such as 3-year total shareholder return) reduce the short-termism problem. Some high-profile CEOs (like Elon Musk and Steve Jobs) accepted $1 annual salaries but held enormous stock positions, making their personal wealth directly dependent on company performance. However, incentive contracts can be abused — managers may manipulate accounting numbers to trigger bonuses or take excessive risks knowing the upside is theirs but downside is borne by shareholders.

### Q: Explain concentrated ownership and how it serves as a substitute for weak investor protection.

**Model Answer**:
In countries with weak legal systems, minority shareholders face a higher risk of expropriation by insiders — managers may steal funds or controlling shareholders may divert resources to themselves. When courts cannot effectively protect investors, concentrated ownership emerges as a private solution: a large shareholder with enough stake (say 20-50%) has both the incentive (significant wealth at stake) and the power (voting control) to monitor management directly.

Three key mechanisms amplify this control beyond simple ownership percentages: First, **shares with superior voting rights** allow a founder or family to retain majority voting control even after selling economic stakes to the public. Second, **pyramidal ownership structures** let a controlling family chain together a series of holding companies, maintaining control of large corporate groups with relatively small capital investment. Third, **interfirm cross-holdings** — common in Japan's keiretsu system — create networks of mutual ownership that entrench control and make hostile takeovers nearly impossible.

The downside is that concentrated ownership simply shifts the agency problem: instead of managers exploiting dispersed shareholders, now controlling shareholders exploit MINORITY shareholders. This is why countries need both legal investor protection AND governance mechanisms that protect all shareholders, not just the controlling block.

### Q: Discuss implications of concentrated ownership across Germany, China, and Japan.

**Model Answer**:
Concentrated ownership takes very different forms across countries, each shaped by its history and legal system.

In **Germany**, banks and insurance companies traditionally held large blocks of corporate shares and served on supervisory boards, directly monitoring management. The two-tier board system (Vorstand for management, Aufsichtsrat for supervision) gives large shareholders formal representation. Workers also have codetermination rights on the supervisory board. This "stakeholder model" means German managers are accountable not just to shareholders but to employees and creditors. Large shareholders (banks, families) have strong incentives to monitor, but minority shareholders have weaker protections. Research shows the 3 largest shareholders in German companies own on average 48% — extremely concentrated versus the US (19%).

In **China**, the government is the dominant large shareholder in most major listed companies, either directly or through state-owned holding companies. This creates a unique form of the agency problem: the "government as shareholder" conflict means political objectives (employment maintenance, regional development, national champions) often override profit maximization. Corporate governance reforms have been implemented, but state control remains dominant. Foreign minority investors in Chinese SOEs face substantial risks.

In **Japan**, the keiretsu system involves complex webs of cross-shareholdings among affiliated companies, typically including a main bank. This mutual ownership entrenches incumbent management (boards are insider-dominated) but also provides stable, patient capital — Japanese firms face less short-term pressure than US firms. The downside is that poor managers are rarely replaced; the threat of hostile takeover is minimal; and minority shareholder returns have historically been low. Post-1990s reforms began breaking up some cross-shareholdings, and foreign activist investors (as in the Toshiba case, 2021) have started challenging this system.

### Q: Explain why firm hedge transaction exposure. Describe the forward contract hedging method.

**Model Answer**:
Firms face transaction exposure when they have contractual cash flows (receivables or payables) denominated in foreign currencies. Without hedging, the domestic currency value of these cash flows is uncertain, creating risk for budgeting, planning, and financial reporting.

Reasons to hedge include: (1) information asymmetry — managers understand the firm's specific exposure better than shareholders; (2) differential transaction costs — firms can hedge at wholesale rates unavailable to individual investors; (3) default costs — hedging reduces earnings volatility and the probability of financial distress, lowering the cost of capital; and (4) progressive tax savings — stable earnings may face lower average tax rates than volatile earnings.

The forward market hedge is the most direct method. For a firm with a foreign currency RECEIVABLE (e.g., Boeing expecting £10M in one year): the firm SELLS pounds forward at the agreed forward rate ($1.46/£). Regardless of what the spot rate is at maturity, Boeing delivers £10M and receives $14.6M. The exchange risk is completely eliminated. The gain or loss from hedging = (Forward Rate − Maturity Spot Rate) × FC Amount. If the pound weakens below $1.46 (e.g., $1.40), the hedge was beneficial (saved $600K). If the pound strengthens above $1.46 (e.g., $1.50), the hedge was costly (gave up $400K upside).

### Q: What are the economic consequences of protecting investor rights?

**Model Answer**:
Strong investor protection creates a virtuous cycle for economic development. When minority investors trust that their rights will be upheld, they are willing to invest in companies they don't control — this leads to more dispersed ownership, broader stock market participation, and lower cost of capital for firms.

Empirically (La Porta et al.), countries with stronger investor protection (typically common law countries like USA, UK, Australia) have: (1) more dispersed corporate ownership — shareholders trust courts to protect them; (2) larger, more liquid capital markets — more investors participate; (3) higher corporate valuations — investors pay a premium for rights they trust; (4) more IPOs and equity market development — firms can raise external capital cheaply; and (5) higher long-term economic growth — capital is allocated more efficiently.

Conversely, weak investor protection (common in civil law countries) leads to concentrated ownership (the only safe way to invest is to BE the controlling shareholder), underdeveloped capital markets, undervalued firms, and slower growth. Countries like Italy (Parmalat) and Brazil (Petrobras) illustrate how weak governance allows insider expropriation at massive scale.

The implication for policy: legal reform improving minority shareholder protection is not just a governance issue — it's an economic development strategy. Capital markets deepen, investment flows increase, and firms can access cheaper financing when investors feel protected.

### Q: Why do firms invest overseas? Explain 3 key reasons.

**Model Answer (from HW9 exam Q10)**:
**Trade Barriers**: When governments impose tariffs, quotas, or other restrictions, exporting goods becomes costly or impossible. Firms respond by setting up production INSIDE the target market, turning a potential export into a local product that bypasses trade barriers entirely. Honda's Ohio factories are the textbook example — producing in the US avoided US import restrictions on Japanese cars.

**Imperfect Labor Market**: The labor market is the most imperfect of all factor markets because workers cannot freely cross international borders due to immigration restrictions. This creates persistent wage differentials that cannot be arbitraged away. A firm in Denmark pays $54/hour in manufacturing wages; the same work costs $0.80/hour in India. Since the workers can't move to Denmark, the factory moves to India. This is fundamentally a response to market imperfection caused by national borders.

**Intangible Assets**: MNCs typically possess proprietary advantages — technologies, brands, management systems — that are their competitive edge. The problem with simply licensing these assets to foreign firms is that the foreign partner might steal or misappropriate them. FDI allows the firm to USE the intangible asset globally while RETAINING ownership and control. This is the "internalization advantage" — it's better to internalize the use of intangible assets within the firm than to sell access to them in imperfect markets. Coca-Cola's withdrawal from India rather than reveal its formula is the ultimate illustration of this logic.

## 1.3 Bid-Ask Spread Calculations

### Setup
Bank always quotes: **Bid / Ask** (Bid < Ask)
- Bank BUYS base currency at **Bid** (lower price — bank pays less)
- Bank SELLS base currency at **Ask** (higher price — bank charges more)
- You always get the WORSE rate (buy high from bank, sell low to bank)

### Example 1: Spot transaction
EUR/USD: Bid = 1.1200 / Ask = 1.1210
Spread = 1.1210 − 1.1200 = 0.0010 ($0.0010 per euro)

You want to BUY €100,000:
- You pay at ASK: €100,000 × $1.1210 = **$112,100**

You want to SELL €100,000:
- You receive at BID: €100,000 × $1.1200 = **$112,000**
- Bank profit = $112,100 − $112,000 = **$100** (per round trip)

### Example 2: Percentage spread
Bid = 1.1200, Ask = 1.1210
% Spread = (Ask − Bid) / Ask × 100 = 0.001/1.121 × 100 = **0.089%**

### Example 3: Retail vs Interbank
If interbank EUR/USD = 1.1235 and retail bank adds 1% spread:
- Retail Ask = 1.1235 × 1.005 = 1.1292 (you pay more buying EUR)
- Retail Bid = 1.1235 × 0.995 = 1.1179 (you receive less selling EUR)

## 1.4 Triangular Arbitrage — Full Numerical Walkthrough

### Concept
Three currencies, three exchange rates. If the cross-rate implied by two rates ≠ the quoted third rate → profit opportunity exists.

### Step-by-Step Method
1. Calculate the **implied cross rate** from two known rates
2. Compare to the **quoted cross rate**
3. If different → arbitrage exists; determine direction
4. Execute: Start with $1M, go around the triangle the profitable way

### Example 1: Classic Triangular Arbitrage
Given:
- USD/EUR = 1.1235 (1 EUR costs $1.1235)
- USD/GBP = 1.3447 (1 GBP costs $1.3447)
- EUR/GBP quoted = 1.2000 (1 GBP costs €1.20)

**Step 1: Calculate implied EUR/GBP**
Implied EUR/GBP = USD/GBP ÷ USD/EUR = 1.3447 / 1.1235 = **1.1969**

**Step 2: Compare**
Quoted = 1.2000 vs Implied = 1.1969
GBP is OVERPRICED in EUR terms (costs more EUR than it should) → buy GBP cheap (via USD), sell GBP expensive (for EUR)

**Step 3: Execute with $1,000,000**
- Start: $1,000,000
- Buy GBP at $1.3447/£: $1,000,000 / 1.3447 = **£743,769**
- Sell GBP for EUR at quoted 1.2000: £743,769 × 1.2000 = **€892,523**
- Sell EUR for USD at 1.1235: €892,523 × 1.1235 = **$1,002,622**
- **Arbitrage Profit = $2,622** (risk-free!)

### Example 2: From TD3 — Forward Rate & Arbitrage Setup
Given (from course slides, TD3 Question 1):
- Spot USD/JPY: Bid = 116.903, Ask = 117.565 (yen per dollar)
- 3-month forward: 116.886

**a. 3-month bid rate for dollars (forward)**
Answer from slides: **117.565** ← spot ask for yen (buy $ sell ¥)

**b. 3-month ask rate for dollars (forward)**
Answer: **117.541**

**c. Is yen at premium or discount?**

→ Dollar costs FEWER yen forward → Dollar is at **DISCOUNT**
→ Yen is at **PREMIUM** (yen appreciates against dollar)

**d. Forward premium on yen (annualized)**
Premium = (Spot − Forward) / Forward × (12/3) × 100
= (117.565 − 116.903) / 116.903 × 4 × 100
= 0.662/116.903 × 4 × 100
= **0.566% per year** ← from slides ✓

**e. From IRP: what is yen interest rate?**
Formula: F/S = (1 + r_yen)/(1 + r_$)
116.903/117.565 = (1 + r_yen)/(1.015) ← 1.5% quarterly dollar rate
0.9944 × 1.015 = 1 + r_yen
r_yen = 0.00928 = **0.928% per quarter** ← from slides ✓

**f. What 3-month spot rate is consistent with PPP?**
Expected spot = 117.565 × (1 + π_US)/(1 + π_Japan)
From slides: **¥117.429 = $1**

**g. Expected inflation differential**
117.429/117.565 − 1 = −0.00116 = **−0.116%**

## 1.5 Forward Premium/Discount

### Formula (annualized)
**Premium/Discount (%) = [(F − S) / S] × (360 / n) × 100**
where n = number of days (or use 12/months)

Positive = forward premium on base currency (base currency expected to appreciate)
Negative = forward discount on base currency (base currency expected to depreciate)

### Example 1: South African Rand (from TD3 Q2)
- Spot: ZAR 11.0976 = $1 (so dollar is base, rand is quote)
- 3-month forward: ZAR 10.9308 = $1
- Forward has FEWER rand per dollar → dollar costs less rand → dollar at **DISCOUNT**, rand at **PREMIUM**

Annual premium on dollar (actually discount):
= 4 × (10.9308/11.0976 − 1)
= 4 × (−0.01503)
= **−6.012%** ← annual discount on dollar vs rand (from slides ✓)

### Example 2: Japanese Yen Forward Rates
From Exhibit 5.7:
- Spot: ¥115.11/$
- 1-month forward: ¥115.09/$
- 3-month forward: ¥114.98/$
- 6-month forward: ¥114.70/$

6-month premium on yen (discount on dollar):
= (2/1) × (115.11 − 114.70)/115.11 × 100
= 2 × 0.00356 × 100 = **0.712% annualized** (dollar at discount)

### Example 3: Swiss Franc
From Exhibit 5.7:
- Spot: CHF 0.9272/$ (or $1.0785/CHF)
- 6-month forward: CHF 0.9211/$ (or $1.0857/CHF)

Dollar forward vs CHF: (0.9211 − 0.9272)/0.9272 × 2 × 100
= −0.00658 × 2 × 100 = **−1.316%** annualized (dollar at discount vs CHF)

## 2.2 Interest Rate Parity (IRP) — Full Numerical Examples

### Formula (when dollar is QUOTE currency, i.e., $/£)
**F = S × (1 + i_$) / (1 + i_£)**

### Formula (when dollar is BASE currency, i.e., ¥/$)
**F = S × (1 + i_¥) / (1 + i_$)**

### Example 1: IRP Check — Do Two Investments Yield Same Return?
(From IF5 slides — Ruritania example)
- US rate: 5% per year
- Ruritanian peso (RUP) rate: 15.5% per year
- Spot: RUP 50 per $1
- 1-year forward: RUP 55 per $1
- Investment amount: $1,000

**US path:**
$1,000 × (1 + 0.05) = **$1,050**

**Ruritanian path:**
Step 1: Convert $1,000 to RUP: $1,000 × 50 = **RUP 50,000**
Step 2: Invest at 15.5%: 50,000 × 1.155 = **RUP 57,750**
Step 3: Convert back at forward rate: 57,750 / 55 = **$1,050**

Both = $1,050 → **IRP holds → No arbitrage** ✓

### Example 2: CIA — Detecting and Exploiting Arbitrage
(From IF5 slides — UK/US example, MOST IMPORTANT)

**Given:**
- US interest rate: i_$ = 5%
- UK interest rate: i_£ = 8%
- Spot: S = $1.80/£
- 1-year forward: F = $1.78/£

**Step 1: Check if IRP holds**
IRP forward rate = S × (1 + i_$)/(1 + i_£) = 1.80 × (1.05/1.08) = 1.80 × 0.9722 = **$1.75/£**
Actual forward = $1.78/£ ≠ $1.75/£ → **IRP VIOLATED → Arbitrage exists!**

Since actual F ($1.78) > IRP F ($1.75):
→ Forward pound is OVERPRICED → Sell pounds forward (lock in selling £ at $1.78)
→ Buy pounds spot (get them cheap at $1.80... wait — borrow in US, invest in UK)

**Step 2: Direction**
UK-adjusted return = (F/S) × (1 + i_£) = (1.78/1.80) × 1.08 = 0.9889 × 1.08 = **1.068**
US return = (1 + i_$) = **1.05**
1.068 > 1.05 → UK investment better → Borrow in US, invest in UK

**Step 3: Execute (with $1,000,000)**
1. Borrow $1,000,000 at 5% in US → owe $1,050,000 in 1 year
2. Buy £555,556 at spot ($1.80/£): $1,000,000 / 1.80 = **£555,556**
3. Invest £555,556 at 8% for 1 year → **£600,000**
4. Sell £600,000 forward at $1.78/£ → **$1,068,000**
5. After 1 year: receive £600,000, deliver to forward → get $1,068,000, repay $1,050,000
6. **Arbitrage profit = $1,068,000 − $1,050,000 = $18,000** ✓

**Step 4: Market adjustments that restore IRP**
| Action | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Borrow in US | US rate ↑ |
| Buy £ spot | Spot £ price ↑ (S↑) |
| Invest in UK | UK rate ↓ |
| Sell £ forward | Forward £ price ↓ (F↓) |
→ All four adjustments push toward IRP equilibrium

# MODULE 3: FUTURES CALCULATIONS

## 3.1 Futures Pricing Formula

### For Currency Futures (same as IRP/forward pricing)
**F = S × (1 + i_domestic)^T / (1 + i_foreign)^T**

For continuous compounding: F = S × e^(r_d - r_f)×T

### For Commodity Futures
**F = S × (1 + r_f − convenience yield + storage cost)^T**

### Example 1: Corn futures pricing (from HW6/TD5 Q3)
- Spot price: S₀ = 95 (cents per bushel)
- Risk-free rate: r_f = 10%
- Convenience yield: y = 8/95 per unit = 0.0842 (per unit term)
- Time: T = 0.5 years (6 months)

**F = S₀ × (1 + r_f − y)^T**
F = 95 × (1 + 0.10 − 8/95)^0.5
F = 95 × (1 + 0.10 − 0.0842)^0.5
F = 95 × (1.0158)^0.5
F = 95 × 1.00787
**F = 95.747** ✓ (from slides)

### Example 2: Currency futures
EUR/USD spot = 1.10, US rate = 3%, Euro rate = 1%, T = 6 months (0.5 years)
F = 1.10 × (1.03/1.01)^0.5 = 1.10 × (1.0198)^0.5 = 1.10 × 1.00985 = **$1.1108/€**

**Short (seller) P&L = (F_open − F_close) × Contract Size**

## 3.3 Hedging with Futures

### Example: Corn Farmer Hedge
Farmer has 30,000 bushels of corn to sell in December.
Current futures: $6.60/bushel (= 660 cents)
Farmer SELLS futures to lock in price.

At maturity:

# MODULE 4: OPTIONS CALCULATIONS

## 4.1 Option Payoff at Maturity

### Formulas
**Call payoff = max(S_T − K, 0)**
**Put payoff = max(K − S_T, 0)**
**Net profit (buyer) = Payoff − Premium × (1 + r)^T** (adjust premium for time value)
**Net profit (seller) = Premium × (1 + r)^T − Payoff** (opposite of buyer)

### Example 1: Call Option
- Stock current price: S₀ = $100
- Call option: Strike K = $105, Premium = $8
- At maturity, S_T = $120

Payoff = max($120 − $105, 0) = **$15**
Net profit for buyer = $15 − $8 = **$7**
Net profit for seller = $8 − $15 = **−$7**

### Example 2: Put Option
- Stock: S₀ = $100
- Put option: Strike K = $95, Premium = $5
- At maturity, S_T = $80

Payoff = max($95 − $80, 0) = **$15**
Net profit for buyer = $15 − $5 = **$10**

- At maturity, S_T = $100

Payoff = max($95 − $100, 0) = **$0** (option expires worthless)
Net profit for buyer = $0 − $5 = **−$5** (lose the premium)

### Example 3: Break-even point
For a call: Break-even S_T = K + Premium = $105 + $8 = **$113**
For a put: Break-even S_T = K − Premium = $95 − $5 = **$90**

## 4.2 Put-Call Parity

### Formula
**C − P = S₀ − PV(K)**
Or: **C + PV(K) = P + S₀**

Where: C = call price, P = put price, S₀ = current stock price, PV(K) = K/(1+r)^T

### Example (from TD7 Q5b)
S₀ = $100, K = $100, r = 10%, T = 1 year
PV(K) = $100/1.10 = $90.91
Minimum call price = S₀ − PV(K) = $100 − $90.91 = **$9.09** ✓ (from slides)

### Example 2: Finding put price from call price
S₀ = $50, K = $50, r = 5%, T = 1 year, Call price = $5
P = C − S₀ + PV(K) = $5 − $50 + ($50/1.05) = $5 − $50 + $47.62 = **$2.62**

## 4.4 Hedging with Options — Full Numerical Example

### The Mexican Peso Put Option (from past paper Q9 — most detailed)

**Setup:**
- US firm sold product; will receive MXN 20,000,000 in 1 year
- Bought PUT options on MXN: Strike = MXN 20/USD (= $0.05/MXN)
- Premium = $0.001 per MXN
- US interest rate = 5%
- At maturity: spot = MXN 18/USD (= $0.0556/MXN)

**Analysis:**
- Put gives right to SELL MXN at MXN 20/USD (= $0.05/MXN)
- Spot rate at maturity = MXN 18/USD (= $0.0556/MXN)
- $0.0556 > $0.05 → Market rate BETTER than strike → **Do NOT exercise put**
- Exercise put: get $0.05/MXN; Market: get $0.0556/MXN → market wins

**Calculation:**
Proceeds from spot conversion:
MXN 20,000,000 / 18 = **$1,111,111.11**

Premium cost (future value):
$0.001 × 20,000,000 = $20,000 paid 1 year ago
FV = $20,000 × 1.05 = **$21,000**

Net proceeds:
$1,111,111.11 − $21,000 = **$1,090,111.11** ✓

**What if spot had been MXN 22/USD (= $0.0455/MXN)?**
Market rate ($0.0455) < strike ($0.05) → **Exercise the put!**
Proceeds = MXN 20,000,000 × $0.05 = **$1,000,000**
Net = $1,000,000 − $21,000 = **$979,000**
Without hedge: 20,000,000/22 = $909,091 → hedge SAVED: $979,000 − $909,091 = **$69,909**

# MODULE 5: EXPOSURE HEDGING — COMPREHENSIVE NUMERICAL

## 5.1 Boeing Example — All Four Hedging Methods Compared

**Setup:**
- Boeing has £10,000,000 receivable in 1 year
- US interest rate: 6.10% p.a.
- UK interest rate: 9.00% p.a.
- Spot rate: $1.50/£
- Forward rate (1-year): $1.46/£

---

### Method A: No Hedge (Unhedged)
Outcome depends entirely on future spot rate S_T:
- If S_T = $1.30: receive £10M × 1.30 = **$13,000,000**
- If S_T = $1.46: receive £10M × 1.46 = **$14,600,000**
- If S_T = $1.50: receive £10M × 1.50 = **$15,000,000**
- If S_T = $1.60: receive £10M × 1.60 = **$16,000,000**
**Risk: Uncertain outcome**

---

### Method B: Forward Market Hedge
**Action**: Sell £10,000,000 forward at $1.46/£

**Outcome**: Receive exactly $14,600,000 regardless of S_T

| S_T | Unhedged | Forward Hedge | Gain from Hedge |
|-----|----------|---------------|----------------|
| $1.30 | $13,000,000 | $14,600,000 | +$1,600,000 |
| $1.40 | $14,000,000 | $14,600,000 | +$600,000 |
| $1.46 | $14,600,000 | $14,600,000 | $0 |
| $1.50 | $15,000,000 | $14,600,000 | −$400,000 |
| $1.60 | $16,000,000 | $14,600,000 | −$1,400,000 |

**Formula**: Gain from hedge = (F − S_T) × £10M = ($1.46 − S_T) × 10,000,000

---

### Method C: Money Market Hedge
**Action**: Borrow pounds today against future receivable, convert to dollars, invest

**Step 1**: Borrow PV of £10M at UK rate:
£10,000,000 / (1 + 0.09) = **£9,174,312** borrowed today

**Step 2**: Convert £9,174,312 to dollars at spot:
£9,174,312 × $1.50/£ = **$13,761,468**

**Step 3**: Invest $13,761,468 at US rate for 1 year:
$13,761,468 × (1 + 0.061) = **$14,600,918 ≈ $14,600,000** ✓

**Step 4**: At maturity, receive £10M, repay pound loan (£9,174,312 × 1.09 = £10,000,000)

**Result**: $14,600,000 — same as forward hedge (IRP ensures this) ✓

---

### Method D: Options Market Hedge
**Action**: Buy put options on £ with strike = $1.46/£

**Assume**: Put premium = $0.02/£ × 10,000,000 = **$200,000** paid upfront
FV of premium = $200,000 × 1.061 = **$212,200**

**Outcomes**:
- If S_T = $1.30: Exercise put → get $14,600,000 − $212,200 = **$14,387,800**
- If S_T = $1.46: At the money → exercise or not = same; net $14,600,000 − $212,200 = **$14,387,800**
- If S_T = $1.50: Don't exercise → get $15,000,000 − $212,200 = **$14,787,800** ← BETTER than forward!
- If S_T = $1.60: Don't exercise → get $16,000,000 − $212,200 = **$15,787,800** ← MUCH BETTER!

**Key advantage**: Options give FLOOR protection but keep UPSIDE (at cost of premium)

---

### Comparing All Methods
| S_T | Unhedged | Forward | Money Market | Options |
|-----|----------|---------|--------------|---------|
| $1.30 | $13,000,000 | $14,600,000 | $14,600,000 | $14,387,800 |
| $1.46 | $14,600,000 | $14,600,000 | $14,600,000 | $14,387,800 |
| $1.50 | $15,000,000 | $14,600,000 | $14,600,000 | $14,787,800 |
| $1.60 | $16,000,000 | $14,600,000 | $14,600,000 | $15,787,800 |

**Conclusions**:
- Forward = Money Market (when IRP holds): Same certain outcome
- Options = Forward when S_T < K (protection floor); Options > Forward when S_T > K (upside kept)
- Unhedged is BEST if pound strengthens, WORST if pound weakens

## 6.1 Classifying Transactions

### Debit vs Credit Rule
| Situation | Account | Sign |
|-----------|---------|------|
| US exports goods | Current Account | Credit (+) |
| US imports goods | Current Account | Debit (−) |
| Foreigner buys US stock | Financial Account | Credit (+) |
| US buys foreign stock | Financial Account | Debit (−) |
| US receives dividends from abroad | Current Account (Primary Income) | Credit (+) |
| US sends remittance abroad | Current Account (Secondary Income) | Debit (−) |
| US central bank sells foreign reserves | Official Reserves | Credit (+) |

### Detailed Example (from TD1 Q12 — all four sub-questions)

**a) Japanese insurance company buys US Treasury bonds, pays from its NYC bank account:**
- Financial Account, Credit (+): US sold financial asset to foreigner → capital inflow
- Financial Account, Debit (−): Reduction in Japanese-owned bank account in US (other investment)
- Net: Zero BoP effect (one FA entry offsets another FA entry), but composition shifts

**b) US citizen dines at Paris restaurant, pays with American Express:**
- Current Account, Debit (−): US imported a service (travel/tourism)
- Financial Account, Credit (+): Increase in US credit card liability (future payment to AmEx → France)
- Net: CA debit offset by FA credit

**c) Indian immigrant in LA sends check to parents in Mumbai:**
- Current Account, Debit (−): Secondary income / unilateral transfer outflow
- Financial Account, Credit (+): Decrease in US bank account held by Indian immigrant
- Net: CA debit (transfer) offset by FA

**d) US programmer hired by British company, paid from British-held US bank account:**
- Current Account, Credit (+): US exported a service (labor/consulting)
- Financial Account, Debit (−): Decrease in UK-owned US bank account balance
- Net: CA credit offset by FA debit

## 7.1 Ownership and Voting Rights

### Example 1: Simple Ownership % (from exam screenshot)
Company A: 10,000 total shares
Investor B: holds 4,000 shares (with 2 votes per share)
Other shareholders: 6,000 shares (1 vote per share)

Economic ownership = 4,000 / 10,000 = **40%**

Voting power:
Investor B votes = 4,000 × 2 = 8,000
Other votes = 6,000 × 1 = 6,000
Total votes = 14,000
Investor B voting % = 8,000 / 14,000 = **57.14%** (majority control!)

Separation: 40% cash flow rights but 57% voting control

### Example 2: Control Value (from exam screenshot)
Dominant investor: 60% voting rights
Company total value: $10,000,000
Investor's debt: $2,000,000

Control value C:
= (Voting %) × (Company Value) − Debt
= 0.60 × $10,000,000 − $2,000,000
= $6,000,000 − $2,000,000
= **C = $4,000,000** ✓

### Example 3: Pyramidal Ownership — Effective Control
Family structure:
- Family directly owns 51% of Holding Co A
- Holding Co A owns 51% of Operating Co B
- Operating Co B owns 51% of Target Co C

Family's EFFECTIVE ownership of C:
= 51% × 51% × 51% = **13.27% economic ownership**

BUT: Family CONTROLS C through majority at each level
→ With only 13.27% of C's cash flows, family makes ALL decisions about C

This is the "separation of ownership and control" through pyramids.

### Example 4: Who really benefits from tunneling?
Family owns 13.27% of C economically but controls 100% of decisions.
If C transfers $1,000,000 to family-controlled entity:
- Family receives $1,000,000
- But only "paid" for via their share: 13.27% × $1,000,000 = $132,700 in lost value
- Minority shareholders bear 86.73% = $867,300 of the loss
- Family NET GAIN from tunneling = $1,000,000 − $132,700 = **$867,300**

This is why minority shareholder protection is crucial.

## 8.1 Full 2-Part Question: FX + IRP

**Q: The spot rate for GBP/USD is 1.3447. The 1-year forward is 1.3435. US interest rate is 2%. What should the UK interest rate be according to IRP? Is there a forward premium or discount on the dollar?**

F = S × (1 + i_US) / (1 + i_UK)
1.3435 = 1.3447 × (1.02) / (1 + i_UK)
(1 + i_UK) = 1.3447 × 1.02 / 1.3435
(1 + i_UK) = 1.3716 / 1.3435
(1 + i_UK) = 1.02090
i_UK = **2.09%**

Interpretation: UK rate (2.09%) is slightly higher than US rate (2%), consistent with pound at slight forward discount.

**Part 2: Forward premium/discount**
(F − S)/S × 100 = (1.3435 − 1.3447)/1.3447 × 100 = −0.089%
Dollar is at forward **DISCOUNT** vs pound (or pound is at slight premium)
Annualized: −0.089% per year
